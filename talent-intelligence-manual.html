A note on definition

TI is both a verb and a noun

Talent Intelligence (proper noun) = the function or centre of excellence. Talent intelligence (noun) = the output and deliverable. Talent intelligence (verb) = the action and process. This manual uses the distinction throughout — it matters more than it appears to, because it determines who owns what and how success is measured.

Foundations · 01

What Is Talent Intelligence?

The unified definition

Two camps have long debated what TI means. Practitioners emphasize external labor market data. Vendors (Eightfold, LinkedIn, etc.) emphasize internal lifecycle data and AI-driven tools. Neither is complete. The most useful synthesis:

Culshaw's Unified Definition (2022)
Talent Intelligence is the augmentation of internal and external people data with the application of technology, science, insights and intelligence relating to people, skills, jobs, functions, competitors and geographies to drive business decisions.

Key phrase: drive business decisions. Not inform. Not support. Drive. This distinction determines what customer level you should be targeting and what KPIs should govern your work.

TI as three things at once

FormUsageExample
Proper nounThe function / platform / field"The Talent Intelligence team will present to leadership."
NounThe deliverable or output"The talent intelligence report shows a 14-month hiring lead time."
VerbThe action / process"By talent intelligencing this market we uncovered competitor org structure."

Why now?

Three forces converged to make TI essential rather than optional:

  • Skills shortage at scale. KornFerry (2018) projected 85 million skilled worker shortages by 2030, representing $8.45 trillion in lost revenue — the combined GDP of Germany and Japan.
  • Accelerated digital transformation. Covid compressed years of workforce change into months; organizations entered new markets needing skills they had never hired for.
  • Labor market data finally aggregated. Vendors like Burning Glass (1999), EMSI (2000), Horsefly (2011), TalentNeuron (2012), Draup (2017), and Stratigens (2018) created the infrastructure for macro-level intelligence that previously required raw government data and specialist economists.
Context signal

The moonwalking bear

Transport for London's 2008 awareness campaign showed viewers counting basketball passes while missing a person in a bear suit moonwalk across screen. Culshaw uses this as the central metaphor for TI: leaders making decisions on autopilot, brain on autopilot, missing the obvious because they're focused elsewhere. TI's job is to make the bear visible — before it becomes a problem.

The intelligence history

Talent Intelligence as a term appeared sporadically from the mid-1990s — initially referring only to analytics on internal talent. The modern external-data form emerged with the second wave of vendor platforms (2017–2019). The 2021 TI Collective survey found that over 50% of existing TI functions had been created in the previous two years.

Toby's Takeaways
  • TI is a new and largely undefined field — practitioners still have the power to shape it
  • There has never been a more relevant time to launch a TI capability
  • The demand pull is real and growing — controlling supply will be your primary constraint
  • Vocabulary discipline matters: be specific about whether you mean the function, the output, or the activity
Foundations · 02

TI vs. Adjacent Functions

"The Great Debate" — Culshaw, Chapter 3

The most common question in the field: What's the difference between Talent Intelligence and HR Analytics? The honest answer is that the lines are blurry and constantly shifting. But the distinctions matter — they determine scope, customer base, KPIs, and team composition. Here's the clearest framework for navigating this.

The six disciplines

Function Primary lens Data orientation Typical customer Key output
HR Analytics (HRA) Internal health & KPIs Internal only HR leadership Attrition, retention, DEI rates
People Analytics (PA) Holistic employee + customer data Internal (expanding) CHRO, board Org health, engagement, culture
Workforce Analytics (WA) Productivity & collaboration Internal, future-facing Business leaders Capacity, flight risk, performance
Talent Intelligence (TI) External labor market External + internal augmented Strategic decision-makers Location, competitor, skills decisions
Sourcing Intelligence (SI) External talent at micro level External (candidate-level) TA leaders, recruiters Sourcing strategy, pipelines
Executive Recruitment Research (ERR) Niche executive market External (individual-level) Exec search partners Org maps, executive profiles
TA Analytics (TAA) Recruiting efficiency Internal (ATS/CRM) TA team Time-to-hire, cost-per-hire, funnel

The clearest distinction

Internally-focused functions (HRA, PA, WA) tend to have stronger data analytics skills and self-service culture, while building consulting skills. Externally-focused functions (TI, SI, ERR) tend to be stronger on consulting and business partnering, while developing data analytics capability.

The core dividing line

Internal vs. external data focus

The most reliable way to map these functions is by their primary data source. HRA/PA/WA start from your own employees and look inward. TI/SI/ERR start from the external labor market and look outward. The bridge function — the one that spans both — is Talent Acquisition Analytics. All functions are expanding their scopes, and future convergence into an "HR Science" model is likely.

How they interact in organizations

Most organizations won't have all of these. Many will have activities covered by other groups, several merged into single functions, or none with clear ownership. This is a transformative period — don't expect clean org charts.

The practical approach: understand what each function is doing in your organization, identify the white space (usually external market context), and use that white space to define your TI remit without stepping on adjacent capabilities. Partner early and often.

Scope discipline

What TI is NOT

TI is not TA efficiency reporting (time to fill, cost per hire). It's not HR health metrics (engagement scores, headcount reporting). It's not candidate ATS tracking. These belong elsewhere. TI's unique value is the external market context — the view from outside that internal teams structurally cannot see themselves.

Toby's Takeaways
  • Many functions are now touching human capital intelligence from different angles — there's enormous overlap
  • Internally-focused teams have stronger analytics skills; externally-focused teams have stronger consulting skills. Know which you are and develop the other
  • Alignment between functions is a big opportunity — but be clear about the differences before trying to merge them
  • All these functions are broadening scope; convergence into a unified "HR Science" function is a likely future state
Foundations · 03

The Outside View

Most organizations have rich internal data on what happened. External labor market data tells you what's possible — and why the internal picture looks the way it does.

Inside vs. outside contrast

Internal data tells youExternal data tells you
Your time-to-hire is 67 daysWhether 67 days is fast or slow for this role in this market
Your offer acceptance rate dropped 12%Whether competitors raised comp, launched in your market, or the talent pool shifted
You lost 18% of engineers last yearWhere they went, what those companies offered, and what the flight risk looks like next year
A location has 2,000 engineers on LinkedInWhat % are actively looking, what skills they actually have, and what demand they're already facing

The 7 external data signals

Job postings

Real-time demand signal. What are competitors hiring for — and what does a sudden volume spike suggest about strategy?

Talent flows

Where talent moves between employers, industries, and geographies. Reveals talent magnets and vulnerability.

Salary benchmarks

Market compensation by role, level, geography. Essential for offer competitiveness and location cost modeling.

Skills supply

How many people carry a specific skill, where they are, how that supply is growing or shrinking.

Candidate sentiment

Glassdoor, Blind, Indeed reviews. What talent thinks of you and your competitors as employers.

Macro labor data

BLS, ILO, national statistics — long-term trends, demographic shifts, retirement cliffs.

OSINT signals

News, patents, executive movement, company filings. Leading indicators of competitor strategy shifts.

Questions internal data can't answer

  • Is our salary competitive or are we bleeding talent to better-paying competitors?
  • If we open a new site in Phoenix, what's the realistic talent pool — and who's already competing for it?
  • Are our diversity hiring targets feasible given who actually exists in this market?
  • What skills are emerging in our industry that we have no internal capability in yet?
  • Should we build, buy, borrow, or automate this capability?

Intelligence disciplines relevant to TI

Culshaw maps military intelligence disciplines to TI practice. The most directly relevant:

OSINT

Open Source Intelligence — intelligence from publicly available sources. Job postings, LinkedIn, news, patents, Glassdoor, company websites. The backbone of most TI work.

HUMINT

Human Intelligence — from direct contact. Every recruiter conversation is an intelligence gathering opportunity. No other function talks to as many competitor employees.

GEOINT

Geospatial Intelligence — location analysis, talent concentration maps, commute shed modeling. Relevant to site selection and location strategy.

HUMINT in practice

Recruiters as intelligence assets

No other function in your organization talks to as many competitor employees as TA does. During a job interview, a candidate from a competitor might mention a new product, a restructure, or a comp change. Most organizations have no process to capture, route, or act on this intelligence. Building a structured competitive intelligence intake into the TA process — even surgical and targeted — is one of the highest-ROI TI investments available.

From raw data to decision-grade intelligence

1

Raw signals

Job postings, LinkedIn profiles, news feeds, government statistics — unstructured, voluminous, noisy

2

Aggregation

Vendor platforms normalize and structure the raw signals — Draup, TalentNeuron, Lightcast, Horsefly, Stratigens

3

4

Honest limits

Data humility

What external data cannot do

External platforms show talent that's visible online — not the full market. A job posting analysis misses internal transfers, campus programs, and referrals. LinkedIn penetration varies dramatically by geography and function. Platform data can lag 30–90 days. No dashboard can capture a toll road that cuts your commute shed in half, or a competitor just announcing a new site next door. Context — from humans on the ground — remains irreplaceable. Use triangulation across at least two vendors plus a primary data source for any decision of consequence.

The Vendor Landscape

The TI platform market grew from ~$6.5B in 2020 to a projected $13B+ by 2028 (CAGR 9.4%). Understanding the landscape helps you choose tools strategically rather than reactively.

Platform categories

Macro LMI Aggregators

Lightcast (EMSI/Burning Glass), TalentNeuron, Horsefly, Draup, Stratigens, Claro — structured labor market intelligence at population scale

Talent Lifecycle Platforms

Eightfold, SeekOut, hireEZ, Entelo — AI-driven internal + external candidate intelligence across the hiring lifecycle

Insights Portals

LinkedIn Talent Insights — talent pool sizing, company benchmarking, skills trends, embedded in existing recruiter workflows

Specialist / Niche

Crunchbase/Pitchbook (M&A intelligence), Owler (competitor data), Glassdoor/Indeed (sentiment), Talkwalker (media monitoring)

HCM / ATS / CRM

Workday, SAP SuccessFactors, Oracle, iCIMS, Greenhouse, Beamery — internal data infrastructure that TI augments with external context

Raw Government Data

BLS O*NET, EU-LFS, ILO — authoritative, free, slow to update, requires significant processing

Selection framework

No single platform covers everything. Culshaw's recommendation: triangulate across at least two vendors and validate with a third external source. Key selection criteria:

  • Geography coverage: Global vendors vary dramatically in depth outside North America and Western Europe
  • Skills taxonomy: Bucketed vs. granular — a "software engineer" bucket is useless if you need RF analog designers specifically
  • Data refresh rate: Understand lag time before relying on any platform for fast-moving decisions
  • User model: Platform only vs. supported workbench — what's your team's analytical capability?
  • Integration: Can it connect to your ATS/HCM for blended internal/external views?

Foundations · 05

Maturity Model

Culshaw's TI maturity model maps the evolution of a function across five levels, tracking functional health, work horizon, data sophistication, operational clarity, and career path development. It's calibrated to the trusted adviser journey.

The trusted adviser path

Before the five levels, understand the four role stages any TI practitioner or team passes through:

Provider

Delivering objective intelligence against defined requests. Reactive, project-by-project. Aligned to SI/sourcing level.

Problem Solver

Root-cause analysis. Cross-functional. Driving decisions, not just informing. Tactical-to-strategic bridge.

Insight Generator

Proactive suggestions. VP-level engagement. Owns complex programs. High impact, limited guidance.

Trusted Adviser

Seat at the strategy table. Challenges leadership. Knows the problem before they do. Global impact.

The unrealized value gap

Most teams are stuck at Provider

Even excellent TI teams making huge impact at the Provider level are leaving significant value on the table. The gap between Provider and Trusted Adviser is where TI functions earn their strategic seat. The path forward: align expectations as you evolve, build skills for the next level, maintain quality throughout, and ruthlessly prioritize high-impact work.

Build · 06

Operating Model

The foundational questions

Before designing your operating model, you need clarity on four things. Get these wrong and everything downstream suffers.

1. Who is your customer? (The most important question)

Anyone who decides they're a stakeholder is one. Customers are different — they're specifically the people whose needs you're trying to satisfy. Culshaw's key test:

"The key phrase is to drive business decisions. Be clear about whether your customers are in fact the drivers of those business decisions — or if they are simply informing those decision makers. If they are simply informing decision makers, I would argue you have the wrong customer base."— Toby Culshaw

The implication: TI teams seated in TA often serve TA as their customer, when the real customer should be the business leader TA is hiring for. This matters because KPIs drive behavior — and TA KPIs (time to fill, cost per hire) will inevitably shape your TI output in ways that limit strategic impact.

Upstream vs. downstream — the oil production metaphor

Culshaw's upstream/downstream model

Go as far upstream as possible

Think of your customer workflow like oil production. Upstream = where decisions are made. Downstream = where these decisions land operationally. If your TI is positioned downstream (reacting to decisions already made), you spend enormous time and resources mitigating the effects of poor upstream decisions. It is far more effective to inject TI upstream — before the decision is set — where you can actually influence the outcome. Every time you're called in after a decision is made to explain why it's not working, that's a downstream failure that could have been prevented.

2. Capability vs. function — be deliberate

Are you building talent intelligence as a side-of-desk activity inside an existing team? Or a dedicated Talent Intelligence function? Both are valid. But the pivot from one to the other requires a hard reset — don't let it happen by accident. If you're moving from capability to function:

  • Redefine the customer base rather than inheriting the previous one
  • Set a new vision, mission, and principles — don't carry over old ones
  • Be explicit about what's in and out of scope

3. Vision, mission, and principles

Vision = why you exist and what you're building toward. Should be ambitious, realistic, and aligned to company goals.

Example: "The Global Talent Intelligence team will enable and empower our leadership with the right market data, analysis and intelligence, at the right time to drive impactful strategic decisions."

Mission = operational mechanism for achieving the vision today.

Example: "Delivery of talent intelligence work will be made to the end customer and decision maker. We will leverage existing relationships with the business via partner teams and inject talent intelligence into the relevant process mechanisms."

Principles / tenets = guardrails for day-to-day decisions. Examples from high-performing teams:

  • We make recommendations based on research and evangelize data-driven decision-making
  • We identify and solve root causes of problems, not their symptoms
  • We prioritize work that results in measurable impact for our customers
  • We provide both self-service and custom TI solutions to our customers globally

4. Loss leader projects — proactive TI

Not all TI projects should come from defined customer requests. Loss leaders are proactive projects with no defined customer or pre-identified red flag — but where you can see the pain coming and want to spark a conversation:

Macro demographic shifts

Retirement cliffs, baby boomer knowledge transfer, pipeline issues that won't manifest for 5 years

Geopolitical talent impact

Wars, visa regulation changes, Brexit-type events affecting your talent pool before leadership is asking

Industry-level shortages

Skills gaps that industry analysts see coming years out but organizations rarely plan for

Competitor pivots

A talent competitor entering your market or changing their hiring strategy — surfacing this before leadership feels it

Loss leaders serve two purposes: they spark conversations internally, and they market your TI capability to levels of the organization you can't yet reach through traditional project request channels.

Build · 07

Where TI Sits in the Org

Where Talent Intelligence reports shapes its KPIs, customer access, resource model, and long-term trajectory. The 2021 TI Collective survey found: over 50% of TI functions reported into Talent Acquisition, but only 22% thought that was the right home.

The five placement options

Option 1: Talent Acquisition

The most common starting point. Natural home for SI work. Close to the sourcing market.

Pros
Natural affinity with sourcing skills. Close to talent market. Recruiters as intelligence assets (HUMINT). Clear initial customer.
Cons
TA KPIs (time-to-fill) can limit strategic horizon. Customer base capped at TA unless intentionally expanded. Short-termism risk.

Option 2: HR Analytics

Strong data infrastructure. Route to senior leadership already established.

Pros
Existing route to strategic stakeholders. Clean data architecture. Can set external context for internal HR metrics. Strong self-service culture.
Cons
Skills mismatch (analytics-heavy vs. consulting-heavy). Different operational rhythms. TI maturity may lag HRA maturity.

Option 3: Executive Recruitment

Natural for OSINT and competitor intelligence. Immediate access to senior leaders.

Pros
Instant senior-level route to market. Competitive intelligence already embedded. Consulting skills aligned. Trust with leadership.
Cons
Micro (individual) focus may limit macro (population) data capability. Tools/systems may not suit macro LMI work. Consider a ringfenced TI sub-team.

Option 4: Strategic Workforce Planning

Natural fit for long-horizon, strategy-aligned TI work.

Pros
Strategic timeframe alignment. Natural demand for external labor market context. Senior decision-maker access. Long-term view.
Cons
SWP functions vary widely in maturity and resources. May not have TI tooling. Less common as a standalone home for TI.

Option 5: Centralized Intelligence

The emerging "future state" preferred by ~20% of TI professionals. Aligned with CI/Market Intelligence functions.

Pros
Breaks silos. Aligned with Competitor/Market/Marketing Intelligence. Programmatic cadence. Pan-org data access. Most strategic positioning.
Cons
Rare in current state. TI functions not yet common in CI teams. Requires org maturity to implement. Institutional will needed.
Culshaw's principle

Nothing is fixed

No matter where your function is placed today, it does not limit your reach or potential impact. Teams evolve; offerings mature; functions realign. It's very unlikely that any TI function created today will have the same organizational home in five years. Be bold about building relationships and customer base beyond your reporting line. Do not be limited by your org chart.

Intelligence cadence: the AoI alignment

One critical advantage of Centralized Intelligence placement: alignment with Always On Intelligence (AoI). Most other intelligence functions (Competitor, Market, Marketing) are designed around programmatic, ongoing work — not project-by-project delivery. AoI bridges this gap, giving TI a natural home in CI frameworks and a regular, structured output cadence that leadership expects.

Core skills for TI practitioners

Ability to find, evaluate, and synthesize publicly available data at pace. Boolean logic, OSINT tools, data triangulation.

Career pathing

TI is still a young enough field that career paths aren't well-defined across the industry. But within high-performing functions, Culshaw identifies two flows:

Inflow — common entry points: sourcing / executive research, HR analytics, management consulting, research firms, workforce planning, economics / data science.

Outflow — TI alumni commonly move into: strategic workforce planning, people analytics leadership, TA leadership, consultant/advisory roles, vendor-side solutions teams. The skills travel well.

Semiconductor / AI talent context

Hiring TI practitioners for deep tech markets

For semiconductor and AI engineering talent markets, TI practitioners need functional fluency beyond standard TA backgrounds: understanding of chip design vs. fab roles, analog vs. digital vs. RF distinctions, the academic pipeline (PhD-heavy fields like quantum, photonics), geographic concentration in specific hubs (TSMC ecosystem, ARM ecosystem), and the IP-sensitivity of competitive intelligence in this sector. Domain knowledge is non-negotiable — a generalist TI analyst will miss the signal in a job posting that a domain expert reads immediately.

External platform selection

Culshaw's recommendation: triangulate across at least two vendors, validate with a third source. No single platform covers all use cases reliably. Key platforms by strength:

Deep US/UK skills taxonomy. Job posting volume and trends. Strong for skills supply/demand modeling.

Global coverage. Strong for location strategy, workforce planning scenarios. Scenario modeling tools.

Draup

AI-powered. Strong for talent segmentation, skills clustering, competitive talent intelligence. Semiconductor/tech depth.

UK-origin. Strong sentiment and market benchmarking. Good for DEI data and candidate behavior.

Location strategy specialist. Strong for global site selection with talent + cost + risk overlay.

Broadest reach globally. Embedded in recruiter workflow. Best for talent pool sizing and company benchmarking.

Data triangulation methodology

For any decision of consequence, use at least three data sources: two platforms that can corroborate each other, plus a primary research element (recruiter intel, candidate surveys, or qualitative interviews). This is especially important for:

  • Location decisions (platform data + local recruiter ground-truth + government data)
  • Salary benchmarking (two platforms + offer decline analysis)
  • DEI feasibility (macro data + targeted sourcing experiments + pipeline analysis)
  • Competitor intelligence (public signals + HUMINT from TA + sentiment data)
Platform limitation

No dashboard captures context

Culshaw's three scenarios illustrate this: a strong site on paper can be undermined by a toll road inside the commute shed; a growing talent pool can be irrelevant if three competitors just announced sites next door; an "engineering" skills count can be misleading if the taxonomy buckets all STEM and hides the scarcity of your specific niche. External data platforms are directional guides — not decision machines. Human judgment and contextual intelligence remain essential at the final stage of any analysis.

Operate · 10

Project Lifecycle

Culshaw's six-phase lifecycle mirrors military intelligence collection (planning → approach → questioning → termination → reporting), adapted for TI's consulting model.

The six phases

1

Needs capture

Understand the business problem, measurable impact, parameters, stakeholders, timeline, and confidentiality level. Never start a project without this documented.

2

Planning & sign-off

Problem statement, deliverables, success criteria, in/out of scope, risks, timeline. Get this signed off in writing before starting research. Never skip this phase.

3

Research

Data gathering using appropriate sources and methods. Apply data confidence model throughout. Work backwards from delivery date to sequence data acquisition.

4

Project delivery

Output format matched to audience: white paper, presentation, model, dashboard. Control the narrative; match format to how stakeholders will consume it.

5

Post-project wash-up

Internal team debrief: what went well, what could improve, what to do differently. This is the most skipped and most important step. No blame, no hierarchy.

6

Stakeholder feedback

NPS, CSAT, qualitative debrief with the customer. Done live/virtually — not by form. Use this session to also business develop and discover upcoming pipeline.

The Data Confidence Model

Borrowed from UK Police Intelligence reporting. Applies three classification layers to every piece of research:

DCC
Document Confidence

Overall confidence in the document as a whole: High (H), Moderate (M), or Low (L)

DSC
Data Source

Confidence in each source used: Reliable (R), Untested (U), or Not Reliable (NR)

DPC
Data Point

Confidence in each data point: 1 (known first-hand) → 5 (suspected false)

Data Point scoring (1–5):

  • 1 — Known directly to the source (first-hand)
  • 2 — Known indirectly, but corroborated by independent source
  • 3 — Known indirectly (told by someone else)
  • 4 — Not known — no means of assessing
  • 5 — Suspected to be false

Applying this model — even directionally — professionalizes your research and builds leadership trust in ways that raw data delivery never will.

Intake barriers — by design

Counterintuitively, Culshaw recommends deliberately creating friction in your project intake. This reduces low-value demand and ensures you work on what matters.

Non-automated intake

An online form without a follow-up call is dangerous — the customer thinks the clock starts when they submit, putting you on the back foot before day one.

Required project sponsor

Projects must have a sponsor at decision-making level who attends intake and delivery meetings. Removes "nice to have" requests.

Measurable business impact value

Customers must articulate the business impact before you engage. This alone eliminates vanity requests and forces real problem-statement thinking.

Prioritization matrix

Apply Eisenhower's framework with TI-specific definitions:

Important = activities leading to TI functional strategic goals. Urgent = activities directly aligned to stakeholder-defined business value. (Escalation ≠ automatically urgent.)

Critical distinction

Battlecards are a program, not a project

This is one of Culshaw's most important operational points. A battlecard is not a deliverable you produce and hand off. It's a living document that requires ongoing primary and secondary data refresh, embedded recruiter intelligence, and periodic deep-dive updates. The moment your battlecards go stale, they reflect poorly on everything your TI function produces. Factor the ongoing maintenance capacity into any battlecard commitment before you launch.

Always On Intelligence

Scalable by design

AoI must be built for scale from the start. A brilliant analysis that can't be run across dozens of competitors simultaneously is not AoI — it's a one-off deep dive. The value of AoI is the radar function: early warning on competitor pivots, talent magnets, new market entries, and organizational shifts — visible to leadership on a regular cadence, before they feel it in their business results.

Skills & Planning

Semiconductor & AI talent specifics

Standard TI frameworks apply — but this domain has unique structural characteristics that require specialist knowledge to interpret correctly.

The fab vs. design divide

Semiconductor talent splits into two mostly non-overlapping populations with different geographies, employers, compensation, and career paths. Conflating them produces garbage analysis.

Scarce analog / RF pools

Analog, RF, and mixed-signal design talent is genuinely rare globally. Supply is concentrated, academic pipeline is thin, and the populations don't respond to standard market mapping approaches.

CUDA / AI accelerator talent

GPU programming and AI accelerator skills have exploded in demand vs. supply. University pipeline lags demand by years. Geographic concentration (Bay Area, Austin, Toronto, Amsterdam) is extreme.

IP sensitivity

Competitive intelligence in this sector is especially sensitive. Job posting analysis revealing R&D investment areas can be commercially sensitive. Treat OSINT work in this domain with heightened ethics consideration.

PhD-heavy sub-markets

Quantum computing, photonics, advanced packaging — small talent pools concentrated in specific universities, national labs, and a handful of companies. Academic relationship intelligence matters.

Ecosystem dependencies

ARM architecture, TSMC process node, EDA tool expertise (Cadence, Synopsys) — market moves by one ecosystem player create talent waves across the rest. Monitor competitor product roadmaps, not just hiring signals.

Governance & Ethics

Data ethics — the five principles

From Harvard's framework for data ethics, applied to TI practice:

1. Ownership & Consent

Never assume comfort with data collection. Processing personal data requires lawful basis. GDPR requires consent to be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous.

2. Transparency

Be clear about what you're collecting, how it will be used, and why. Deception about methods or intent is unlawful and damages trust.

3. Privacy

Even with consent, data subjects expect privacy. De-identify datasets wherever possible. Breaches hurt individuals and destroy organizational credibility.

4. Intention

Ask: why do we need this? What will change as a result? Is the minimum viable data collection? Good intentions don't automatically make data collection ethical.

5. Outcomes

Consider disparate impact — unintentional discrimination from data analysis. Think through worst-case misuse scenarios before deploying any tool or model.

GDPR for OSINT practitioners

By its nature, OSINT work surfaces personally identifiable information (PII). If you or your target is in the EU, GDPR applies. Key obligations:

  • Have a lawful basis for processing personal data before storing any individual's information
  • Even publicly available data (LinkedIn, news) still requires consent or legitimate interest as the lawful basis
  • Understand whether you're the data controller or data processor — different obligations apply
  • Honor data subject rights: access, erasure, portability
  • Engage your legal and data privacy teams early in any program involving individual-level data
Culshaw's warning

Just because you can, doesn't mean you should

You could theoretically scrape LinkedIn photos and infer ethnicity for DEI analysis. You could use name analysis to infer gender. You could aggregate candidate location data to build commute profiles. All of these are technically possible. None of them are ethical or, in most jurisdictions, legal. The diversity data gap in most markets outside the US is real — but filling it through inference from personal data is not the answer. Be very clear about the ethics of your diversity intelligence work before you build it.

Measurement

Culshaw's central warning

KPIs drive behaviors — choose carefully

Setting "number of projects completed" as a KPI tells your team to move fast over moving well. It incentivizes low-hanging fruit over strategic impact, parallel overload over quality delivery. Whatever you measure will shape how your team allocates time and energy. Set KPIs that drive the behaviors you want — not the ones that are easiest to count.

Metrics vs. KPIs

MetricsKPIs
PurposeMonitor health and process flowMeasure progress against strategic targets
CharacterBusiness-as-usual; often static; industry benchmarks existLaser-focused; SMART; reset periodically
DriverOperational visibilityBehavioral change and goal alignment
ExampleProject overrun rate, average project durationBusiness-articulated financial impact, cost avoidance vs. research firms

Sourcing Intelligence metrics

Common starting metrics for SI teams (use selectively — definitions matter):

  • Total requisitions impacted by TI research
  • Placements where TI directly influenced the hire
  • Time savings vs. recruiter self-research baseline
  • Cost avoidance vs. external research firm rates
  • Recruitment marketing spend reduction tied to TI-informed channel decisions

TI Consulting KPIs (recommended)

For strategic TI functions, Culshaw recommends impact-oriented KPIs rather than activity metrics:

Business-articulated financial impact

The value the customer assigned to the decision the research informed. Captured at intake.

Cost avoidance vs. research firms

Research firm daily rates × project headcount × days. Highly credible to finance stakeholders.

Customer expansion

Number of projects in new customer groups — measures TI's reach into the organization.

Levelling

Number of projects at target decision-making level. Tracks whether TI is reaching the right stakeholders.

Repeat business

Senior stakeholders returning with follow-on work signals trust — the most important leading indicator.

NPS / CSAT

Stakeholder satisfaction score. Best collected live, not by form — completion rate and richness are dramatically higher.

Data capture infrastructure

Build your tracking system from day one — even if you're a team of one. Track at minimum:

Stakeholders & Communications

"A communications strategy would sound strange at first in a talent intelligence team — but I think it is arguably the single biggest missed opportunity for most teams." — Toby Culshaw

Build trust — there are no shortcuts

The path to trusted adviser status is built on consistent, high-quality delivery over time. Culshaw's trust-building principles:

  • Have a brand. Every TI team should have a name, visual identity, and tone that distinguishes their output from all others — internally and externally. Brand = credibility + memorability.
  • Tell your story. How you came about, what you stand for, what you're building. Your customers should know your mission before they get their first deliverable.
  • Be open, honest, and transparent. If you drop the ball — and you will — own it. Your customers are on your side; they want you to succeed too. Openness recovers trust faster than silence.
  • Put a face to the name. A "meet the team" page or bio section in every newsletter. Remote work makes this more important, not less.
  • Ask for feedback and act on it. Asking shows you care. Acting shows you listened. Both matter.
  • Give customers what they need, not what they want. Being a trusted adviser sometimes means challenging the framing of a request. Don't sacrifice the long-term strategic relationship for short-term delivery comfort.

Communications strategy

Two tracks: internal (team rhythm) and external (stakeholders).

Internal communications

  • Consistent meeting cadence and agendas (team reviews, capacity planning)
  • Workload visibility across the team at all times
  • Knowledge management: where research lives, how it's tagged and retrieved
  • Wash-up log and learning capture from every project

External communications

  • Labor market newsletters: Monthly or bi-monthly. Targeted to a curated stakeholder list. Include team updates, market trends, events. Track open rates and adjust.
  • Project updates: Regular comms on project pipeline and delivered work — not just at review meetings
  • Awareness campaign: Proactive internal marketing to raise awareness of TI's capabilities and expand the customer base
  • Business reviews (WBR/MBR/QBR): Structured review of function health, project highlights/lowlights, strategic obstacles, roadmap forward. Done live — email alone doesn't build the relationship

Business review agenda template

Recommended QBR structure

Standard business review agenda

  1. Executive summary — where are we vs. last period?
  2. Goals update — KPIs and strategic goal progress
  3. Project highlights — what delivered well and what impact was measured
  4. Lowlights / learnings — what didn't work and what we changed
  5. Strategic obstacles and challenges — what's in the way?
  6. Roadmap / path forward — what's coming and what do we need?

Build Roadmap

Building TI is not a sprint. The roadmap below reflects Culshaw's phased approach — from scrappy beginnings to trusted adviser status. Use it as a framework, not a prescription.

Phase 1: Experimental (0–6 months)

Jack-of-all-trades phase
  • Define customer base and initial scope — resist inheriting the previous team's default
  • Draft vision, mission, and 3–5 team principles
  • Run 2–3 pilot projects: one quick win for trust, one loss-leader for aspiration, one strategic to set the tone
  • Choose and onboard your first external LMI platform
  • Build a basic project intake and tracking process (even in a spreadsheet)
  • Establish stakeholder feedback mechanism from day one

Phase 2: Tactical (6–18 months)

Building foundations
  • Implement the Data Confidence Model into project documentation
  • Formalize prioritization matrix and intake barriers (non-automated intake, sponsor requirement)
  • Define and launch initial metrics dashboard (project tracking, impact value)
  • Build team brand: name, newsletter, communications cadence
  • Add second LMI platform and implement triangulation methodology
  • Expand customer base beyond initial group — start customer expansion tracking

Phase 3: Operational (18 months – 3 years)

Consistent, impactful delivery
  • Launch Always On Intelligence program for top 5–10 competitors
  • Build battlecard program (ensure refresh capacity exists before launching)
  • Formalize KPIs aligned to business impact, not activity
  • Establish QBR cadence with all key customer groups
  • Develop career paths for TI roles — contribute to the inflow pipeline
  • Publish first loss-leader report to expand organizational awareness

Phase 4–5: Strategic → Transformational

Trusted adviser status
  • TI present in all major strategic planning conversations — not as a support function but as a co-author
  • Future horizons capability: economists and business analysts forecasting 18-month+ talent headwinds
  • Pan-silo data integration: TI layer on top of finance, real estate, HR, and external market data
  • Centralized intelligence consideration: evaluate alignment with CI/Market Intelligence functions
  • TI recognized externally as industry benchmark (award submissions, TI Collective participation, industry speaking)
